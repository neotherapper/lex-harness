# Greek Court Authority & Citation Hierarchy

A reference for what a civil-law claimant can cite in Greek court, and what weight each source carries. This document is jurisdiction-agnostic as to party role (claimant or respondent); it describes the courts, regulators, and doctrinal sources and how Greek courts treat each.

---

## Section 1: Greek Court Hierarchy and Precedent Rules

### 1.1 Is Greek Law "Stare Decisis"?

**NO.** Greece operates under the civil-law tradition (ηπειρωτικό δίκαιο), inherited from Roman law via the German BGB model that shaped the Αστικός Κώδικας (1946). Unlike England or the United States, Greek courts are not formally bound by prior decisions — even from the Supreme Court.

What operates instead is **νομολογία** (case-law tradition): a body of consistent judicial decisions that lower courts are expected to follow as a matter of professional practice, judicial comity, and systemic predictability. In practice, a lower court that ignores consistent ΑΠ jurisprudence risks reversal on appeal.

The distinction matters practically:

- Submissions should frame ΑΠ decisions as "confirmed by consistent Supreme Court jurisprudence" (not "binding precedent")
- A single ΑΠ decision is persuasive; 3+ consistent ΑΠ decisions in the same direction are near-impossible to ignore
- Ολομέλεια ΑΠ decisions are treated in practice as near-binding by all lower courts

### 1.2 Full Court Hierarchy Table

| Supreme Court (section) | Άρειος Πάγος (ΑΠ) | Not formally binding | Very High | YES — standard practice | Most important source for civil-law arguments |
| Council of State | Συμβούλιο της Επικρατείας (ΣτΕ) | Not binding (different jurisdiction) | Medium for regulatory matters | Rarely — only for regulatory/administrative-law interpretation | Different judicial branch; rarely useful in a private civil dispute |

### 1.3 Cross-Circuit Citations

**Question:** Can a party cite decisions from other circuits (ΕφΘεσ = Thessaloniki, ΕφΠειρ = Piraeus)?

Yes, with appropriate framing. Greek law does not restrict citation to same-circuit decisions. In practice:

- **ΕφΠειρ (Piraeus Court of Appeal):** Frequently cited in Athens proceedings without objection. Piraeus handles many commercial and tenancy disputes; its decisions on rental law and consumer protection are regularly accepted by Athens courts.
- **ΕφΘεσ (Thessaloniki Court of Appeal):** Cited less frequently but entirely permissible, especially where no Athens or Piraeus precedent exists on point.
- **ΕφΔωδ, ΕφΛαρ, ΕφΠατρ (other circuits):** Rare but permissible; useful when the legal point is novel and only a peripheral circuit has addressed it.

**Framing for cross-circuit citation:**
> "Σε ανάλογο πλαίσιο, το Εφετείο Πειραιώς κρίνει ότι... [αρ. απόφασης, χρονολογία]."

**Priority order:**
1. ΑΠ (any section) → 2. ΕφΑθ (for Athens) → 3. ΕφΠειρ → 4. ΜονΠρΑθ → 5. Other circuits

## Section 2: EU Court Decisions — Binding Authority

### 2.1 Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU)

**Status: DIRECTLY BINDING on Greek courts where EU law applies.**

When the CJEU interprets an EU regulation (such as the GDPR, which is directly applicable in all Member States), that interpretation is binding on every Greek court, including the Ειρηνοδικείο. Greek courts cannot depart from it. This authority stems from Art. 28 of the Greek Constitution, which integrates ratified international instruments and EU law into the domestic legal order with supra-legislative rank.

**Why the Regulation vs. Directive distinction matters:**

- A **Regulation** (like the GDPR) applies directly without transposition. CJEU interpretations of Regulations are therefore more immediately binding than interpretations of a Directive's transposed national law.
- A **Directive** is transposed through national legislation; CJEU interpretations bind through the principle of conforming interpretation (indirect effect) and, where conditions are met, direct effect against the State.

**How to cite CJEU decisions:**

```
CJEU, [Date], Case C-XXX/YY, [Party v. Party], ECLI:EU:C:[Year]:[Number], para. [X].
```

Example:

```
CJEU, 20 December 2017, Case C-434/16, Peter Nowak v Data Protection Commissioner,
ECLI:EU:C:2017:994, para. 34.
```

**Accessible at:** curia.europa.eu (full text, all languages including Greek) and EUR-Lex (CELEX numbers).

### 2.2 Representative CJEU Decisions on GDPR Access Rights

These decisions are representative of the CJEU's approach to GDPR Art. 15 and related provisions. They are frequently cited in Greek civil proceedings involving data-subject rights.

| Case | CELEX | Key Holding |
|------|-------|-------------|
| C-434/16 Nowak | 62016CJ0434 | Examination scripts = personal data; the "relating to" test is broad |
| C-203/22 | 62022CJ0203 | Blanket GDPR refusals prohibited; controller must explain what it searched |
| C-487/21 | 62021CJ0487 | Art. 15(3): right to receive copies of actual documents, not just summaries |
| C-579/21 Pankki S | 62021CJ0579 | Using data against a person while withholding that data from them violates the GDPR |
| C-154/21 | 62021CJ0154 | Right to identify specific recipients, not merely categories |
| C-307/22 | 62022CJ0307 | Proportionality of access rights; medical data access scope |

**Practical deployment:** In regulatory (HDPA) complaints, lead with CJEU citations. In court, use them to frame the legal standard and then cite HDPA decisions that applied the same standard.

### 2.3 European Court of Human Rights (ECHR)

**Status:** Greece must comply — not formally binding like the CJEU but constitutionally mandated via Art. 28 Syntagma.

Art. 28 provides that ratified international conventions (including the ECHR) form part of Greek law and supersede ordinary legislation. The ECHR is ratified; its jurisprudence must be taken into account by all Greek courts.

**Relevant ECHR provisions for civil disputes:**

- **Art. 8 ECHR** (Right to private life): covers personal data, home, correspondence. Overlaps with the GDPR.
- **Art. 1 Protocol 1 ECHR** (Protection of property): may engage in extreme property-deprivation cases.
- **Art. 6 ECHR** (Fair trial, access to evidence): relevant where a party withholds evidence used against the opponent.

**Citation format:**

```
ECtHR, [Party v. Country], App. No. [XXXXX/YY], [Date], para. [X].
```

**Accessed at:** hudoc.echr.coe.int (free, searchable by article and keyword).

**Practical note:** ECHR arguments are generally secondary to CJEU in GDPR contexts. The CJEU is more specific and more directly binding. Use the ECHR to reinforce, not lead.

### 2.4 General Court of the EU

Handles challenges to EU-institution acts; not relevant to private civil disputes between individuals and companies.

## Section 3: Other EU Member-State Court Decisions

### 3.1 The Core Principle

**Foreign national-court decisions are NOT binding in Greek courts.** Civil-law countries do not recognize foreign precedent as a source of law. A German BGH decision does not bind a Greek Ειρηνοδικείο any more than a Greek ΑΠ decision binds a German Amtsgericht.

**However, there is a critical exception for EU law:**

Where the argument concerns the interpretation of EU law (GDPR, consumer-protection Directives), a foreign court's interpretation of the same EU text carries comparative persuasive value, because the law being interpreted is the same text in all Member States and uniform interpretation is a core EU-law principle.

> "The uniform application of EU law across Member States requires consistent interpretation. The German Federal Court of Justice has interpreted Art. 15 GDPR as requiring... This reading aligns with the CJEU's approach in C-XXX and should be adopted here."

### 3.2 Country-by-Country

| Court | Jurisdiction | Cite for EU Law? | Cite for Greek National Law? | Notes |
|-------|-------------|------------------|------------------------------|-------|
| BGH (Bundesgerichtshof) | Germany | YES — for GDPR/consumer-Directive interpretation | NO | Most active GDPR case law in the EU; high-quality reasoning |
| Cour de cassation | France | YES — for GDPR/consumer-Directive interpretation | NO | Extensive Art. 15 GDPR case law; different legal tradition |
| Hoge Raad | Netherlands | YES — for GDPR/EU law | NO | Dutch DPA has issued strong GDPR enforcement; courts follow |
| OGH (Oberster Gerichtshof) | Austria | YES — for GDPR/EU law | NO | Austria has one of the highest GDPR litigation densities in the EU |
| UK Supreme Court | Post-Brexit UK | NO — UK no longer EU law | NO | UK GDPR is a separate instrument post-Brexit; not applicable |
| EFTA Court | Norway/Iceland/Liechtenstein | Only if EEA law is relevant | NO | Rarely relevant; the GDPR applies via the EEA Agreement |

### 3.3 Framing Foreign Citations Correctly

Never drop a foreign court citation as if it were authority. Frame it as comparative interpretation:

**Template (English):**

> "In analogous circumstances, the German Federal Court of Justice (BGH) interpreted Art. 15 GDPR as requiring the controller to provide copies of actual documents rather than summaries [BGH, Urt. v. XX.XX.XXXX – Az. VI ZR XXX/XX]. While not binding in Greek courts, this interpretation aligns with the purpose of the GDPR as confirmed by the CJEU in C-487/21, and supports the conclusion that the controller's refusal to provide copies violates Art. 15."

**Template (Greek):**

> "Σε ανάλογο ζήτημα, το Ανώτατο Ομοσπονδιακό Δικαστήριο της Γερμανίας (BGH) ερμήνευσε το άρθρο 15 GDPR... Η ερμηνεία αυτή, μολονότι δεν δεσμεύει τα ελληνικά δικαστήρια, συνάδει πλήρως με τη νομολογία του ΔΕΕ (υπόθεση C-487/21)."

## Section 4: Regulatory and Administrative Authority

### 4.1 HDPA (Αρχή Προστασίας Δεδομένων Προσωπικού Χαρακτήρα)

**Status:** Binding administrative decisions under GDPR Art. 83; strong persuasive authority in court.

The HDPA is Greece's competent supervisory authority under GDPR Art. 51. Its decisions:

- Bind the parties to the administrative proceeding
- Carry very high persuasive weight in court for GDPR questions; the court will typically defer to the specialist regulator's interpretation
- Can impose fines of up to €20 million or 4% of global turnover (GDPR Art. 83)
- A favorable HDPA decision before court proceedings becomes powerful evidence of unlawful conduct

**How to cite HDPA decisions:**

```
ΑΠΔΠΧ [Αρ. Απόφασης]/[Έτος], [brief description of facts], [operative para.].
```

**Accessible at:** www.dpa.gr (Greek DPA official website — decisions published in full).

**Strategic note:** File the HDPA complaint before court proceedings where possible. A pending or completed HDPA investigation strengthens a civil case; a favorable HDPA decision can become near-conclusive evidence for the court.

### 4.2 Συνήγορος Καταναλωτή (Consumer Ombudsman)

**Status:** Recommendations are NOT binding on courts but carry significant persuasive weight.

The Consumer Ombudsman operates under Law 3297/2004 and issues recommendations (Πορίσματα) in consumer disputes. Courts are not required to follow them but frequently consider them because:

- The Ombudsman is a specialist public authority examining consumer-protection law
- A finding that a trader violated Law 2251/1994 is an expert administrative assessment
- It demonstrates that the consumer exhausted ADR before litigation (relevant for proportionality / good-faith arguments)

**Citation format:**

```
Συνήγορος Καταναλωτή, Πόρισμα [Αρ.]/[Έτος], [brief description].
```

### 4.3 ΑΑΔΕ (Ανεξάρτητη Αρχή Δημοσίων Εσόδων — Greek Tax Authority)

**Status:** Binding for tax matters; persuasive for courts on invoice validity and stamp-duty questions.

Representative ΑΑΔΕ instruments:

- **Circular E.2094/2025** — DTD implementation under N. 5177/2025; defines valid electronic-invoice requirements
- **N. 4308/2014 Art. 9** — required invoice content, including recipient ΑΦΜ
- **N. 5177/2025 Art. 16** — stamp-duty abolition (operative from 01/12/2024)
- **myDATA FAQ** — ΑΑΔΕ-permitted placeholder rules for non-EU recipients

**Citation format:**

```
ΑΑΔΕ Εγκύκλιος Ε.ΧΧΧΧ/ΧΧΧΧ, [title], [operative section].
N. [XXXX/XXXX] Art. [X], ΦΕΚ [Α/Β] [XX]/[XX]/[XXXX].
```

### 4.4 European Data Protection Board (EDPB)

**Status:** NOT formally binding but treated as highly authoritative by all EU courts and DPAs.

The EDPB issues Guidelines, Recommendations, and Opinions that interpret the GDPR uniformly across Member States. While technically soft law, their authority is such that:

- The HDPA is expected to follow EDPB guidelines
- Greek courts regularly cite EDPB guidelines when interpreting GDPR provisions
- Departing from an EDPB guideline requires explanation by the authority or court

**Representative EDPB guidelines:**

| Guideline | Relevance |
|-----------|-----------|
| Guidelines 01/2022 on data-subject rights (Art. 15-22) | Scope of Art. 15 access right; what "copies" means |
| Guidelines 09/2022 on personal-data breach notification | Breach notification obligations |
| Guidelines 07/2020 on concepts of controller and processor | Controller/processor delineation and successor liability |

**Citation format:**

```
EDPB, Guidelines [XX/XXXX] on [subject], adopted [date], version [X.X], para. [X].
```

**Accessed at:** edpb.europa.eu/our-work-tools/our-documents/guidelines_en

## Section 5: Academic Legal Doctrine (Νομική Θεωρία)

### 5.1 Status in Greek Courts

Academic doctrine (θεωρία, δογματική) is regularly cited in Greek legal submissions — both by lawyers and, more rarely, by courts in their reasoning. It is NOT binding. Its function is:

- To provide interpretive context for statutory provisions
- To demonstrate that an argument reflects mainstream legal thinking, not a fringe position
- To fill gaps where there is no applicable ΑΠ decision

**Key distinction:** Lawyers cite doctrine in their pleadings (υπομνήματα) frequently. Courts cite it in their reasoning rarely and usually implicitly. Doctrine gives a submission academic grounding but cannot substitute for case law.

### 5.2 Standard References by Area

**Civil Law (Αστικό Δίκαιο):**

| Author | Work | Area |
|--------|------|------|
| Απόστολος Γεωργιάδης | Εμπράγματο Δίκαιο / Ενοχικό Δίκαιο | Core civil law; Art. 281, 288, 592 AK interpretation |
| Μιχαήλ Σταθόπουλος | Γενικό Ενοχικό Δίκαιο | Obligations; good-faith standard |
| Ιωάννης Καράκωστας | Δίκαιο Καταναλωτή | Consumer-protection law; Law 2251/1994 |
| Ευάγγελος Περάκης | Εμπορικές Εταιρείες | Corporate law; spin-off (N. 4601/2019) |

**Civil Procedure (Πολιτική Δικονομία):**

| Author | Work | Area |
|--------|------|------|
| Κεραμεύς / Κονδύλης / Νίκας | Ερμηνεία ΚΠολΔ | Standard procedural commentary |
| Νίκος Νίκας | Πολιτική Δικονομία | Burden of proof; Art. 338-340 KPolD |

**Consumer Law:**

| Author | Work | Area |
|--------|------|------|
| Ιωάννης Καράκωστας | Δίκαιο Καταναλωτή | ΓΟΣ, unfair commercial practices |
| Μ. Δεληγιάννης / Φ. Δωρής | Ενοχικό Δίκαιο | Obligations and defects |

**GDPR / Data Protection:** This field is still developing. Key resources include the Greek law reviews Επιθεώρηση Αστικού Δικαίου (ΕφΑΔ), Νομικό Βήμα (ΝοΒ), and Αρμενόπουλος. Greek commentary on GDPR is sparse; prioritize CJEU and HDPA over academic doctrine for GDPR arguments.

### 5.3 Using Doctrine Effectively

**Do use doctrine for:**

- Explaining the meaning of open-textured provisions (e.g., "φυσιολογική φθορά" under Art. 592 AK — Γεωργιάδης + ΑΠ 985/2020)
- Establishing that Art. 288 AK good faith is violated by systematic deception (Σταθόπουλος)
- Explaining elements of unjust enrichment under Art. 904 AK
- Framing the burden-of-proof standard under Art. 338-340 KPolD (Νίκας)

**Do NOT use doctrine as a substitute for:**

- Case law (always prefer ΑΠ over textbook when both are available)
- Statutory text (always cite the article number, not just the commentary)

**Citation format:**

```
Α. Γεωργιάδης, Ενοχικό Δίκαιο, [Τόμος], [Έτος], σελ. [ΧΧΧ].
Μ. Σταθόπουλος, Γενικό Ενοχικό Δίκαιο, [Έτος], [§ αρ.], αρ. [ΧΧ].
```

## Section 6: Evidence vs. Legal Authority — Critical Distinction

### 6.1 The Three Categories

Understanding this distinction prevents the common error of conflating "we have evidence of X" with "X is legally established."

**Category 1: Legal Authority (Αυθεντία)**

What the law says and how courts have interpreted it. Includes:

- Greek statutes (νόμοι) — primary source; always cite article and ΦΕΚ
- ΑΠ / Εφετείο decisions — νομολογία
- CJEU decisions — binding EU law
- HDPA decisions — regulatory interpretation
- EDPB guidelines — soft law
- Academic doctrine — persuasive interpretation

**Category 2: Evidence (Αποδεικτικά Μέσα)**

Admissible evidence types in Ειρηνοδικείο under Art. 339 KPolD:

| Evidence Type | Greek Term | Notes |
|--------------|-----------|-------|
| Documents | Έγγραφα | Primary evidence type; Art. 339 KPolD |
| Party examination under oath | Εξέταση διαδίκου | Can be requested by either party |
| Witnesses | Μάρτυρες | Must attend in person; affidavit insufficient |
| Expert report (court-appointed) | Πραγματογνωμοσύνη | Court can appoint; parties can request |
| Expert opinion (party-commissioned) | Γνωμοδότηση | Lower weight than court-appointed; useful for valuations |
| Documentary admissions | Ομολογία | Very strong — the opponent's own written statements |
| Photographs | Φωτογραφίες | Admissible; EXIF dates provide documentary authentication |
| Sworn affidavits | Ένορκες βεβαιώσεις | Admissible in Greek civil proceedings |
| Prior judgments (same parties) | Δεδικασμένο | Binding as between the same parties on the same issue |

**Category 3: Expert Reports — Specific Use Cases**

Parties can commission (or request the court to appoint) experts for matters such as:

- **Valuation / depreciation:** an accountant or appraiser calculating depreciated values of fixtures and movables
- **Invoice-compliance audit:** an accountant confirming compliance with N. 4308/2014 and stamp-duty rules under N. 5177/2025
- **Construction / renovation cost assessment:** an engineer or contractor providing an independent cost evaluation

A party-commissioned opinion (γνωμοδότηση) carries less weight than a court-appointed expert (πραγματογνωμοσύνη) but is admissible. The court may appoint its own expert if the parties' reports conflict.

### 6.2 Admissibility Thresholds

In Ειρηνοδικείο proceedings (small claims, simplified procedure):

- Evidentiary formalities are somewhat relaxed compared to full Πρωτοδικείο proceedings
- Documents must be authentic but need not be notarized unless disputed
- Official written communications (e.g., GDPR responses) are directly admissible
- Bank records are documentary evidence — directly admissible
- Digital exports (WhatsApp, email) are admissible as digital documents; authenticity may be challenged

**Key principle (Art. 339 KPolD):** Greek civil courts apply **free assessment of evidence** (ελεύθερη εκτίμηση αποδείξεων) — the judge evaluates the totality of the evidence without being bound by a hierarchy of proof, except that certain facts require specific evidence forms (e.g., contracts above certain amounts require written form).

## Section 7: What NOT to Cite

### 7.1 Never in Court Submissions

| Source | Why Not |
|--------|---------|
| Google Maps reviews | Not legal authority; inadmissible hearsay |
| Consumer forums / complaint boards | Non-legal; unverifiable; no legal standing |
| Booking.com / Agoda listing text | Commercial advertising; not legal authority |
| Wikipedia | Not an authoritative source in any legal system |
| Non-peer-reviewed blog posts | Insufficient authority; may be wrong |
| UK Supreme Court / UK law post-Brexit | No longer EU Member State; different legal system |
| Foreign national tenancy/consumer law (non-EU-harmonized) | Not applicable to Greek national-law questions |
| Dead or parked legal domains | Always verify the source URL resolves and is canonical |

### 7.2 Court vs. Ombudsman — Different Rules

Some sources that are inadmissible or weak in court are appropriate for Consumer Ombudsman or HDPA proceedings:

| Source | In Court | In Ombudsman/HDPA | In Negotiation/Media |
|--------|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Foreign comparative benchmarks | Tier 2 only (negotiation) | Can reference | YES |
| Academic articles (non-standard journals) | Weak but permissible | Acceptable | YES |
| OSINT pattern analysis (trader portfolio) | Admissible as business-pattern evidence | Fully usable | YES |
| Consumer reviews / complaint patterns | NOT admissible | Background only | YES |
| EDPB Guidelines | Admissible (soft law) | Effectively binding on HDPA | YES |
| Ombudsman recommendations (other cases) | Persuasive (not binding) | Binding on the same issues | YES |

## Section 9: Citation Formats — Quick Reference

### Greek Court Decisions

```
ΑΠ [αρ.]/[έτος] (standard form)
ΑΠ Ολομ. [αρ.]/[έτος] (full bench)
ΕφΑθ [αρ.]/[έτος]
ΜονΠρΑθ [αρ.]/[έτος]
```

Example: `ΑΠ 777/2022` or `ΕφΑθ 1234/2021`

### CJEU Decisions

```
ΔΕΕ, [ημερομηνία], υπόθεση C-XXX/YY, [Μέρη], ECLI:EU:C:[έτος]:[αρ.], σκέψη [Χ].
```

Example: `ΔΕΕ, 20.12.2017, C-434/16, Nowak κατά DPC, ECLI:EU:C:2017:994, σκέψη 34.`

### HDPA Decisions

```
ΑΠΔΠΧ [αρ.]/[έτος], [περιληπτική περιγραφή].
```

### ECHR Decisions

```
ΕΔΔΑ, [Μέρη] κατά [Χώρα], Αρ. Προσφυγής [XXXXX/YY], [ημερομηνία], § [Χ].
```

### EDPB Guidelines

```
EDPB, Κατευθυντήριες Γραμμές [XX/XXXX], [τίτλος], [ημερομηνία], παρ. [Χ].
```

### German BGH (comparative use)

```
BGH, Urt. v. [DD.MM.YYYY], Az. [VI ZR XXX/XX], Rn. [X] (συγκριτική αναφορά).
```

### Greek Statutes

```
Άρθρο [Χ] Ν. [ΧΧΧΧ]/[ΧΧΧΧ] (ΦΕΚ Α' [αρ.]/[ημερομηνία]).
```

Example: `Άρθρο 65 §4 Ν. 4601/2019 (ΦΕΚ Α' 44/09.03.2019).`

### Academic Doctrine

```
[Επώνυμο], [Τίτλος], [Έτος], σελ. [ΧΧΧ] / [§ αρ.].
```

## Section 10: Forum-Specific Weight

### What Strengthens a Court Brief

1. ΑΠ decisions on the governing substantive provision
2. CJEU GDPR decisions (binding; no counter-argument if EU law applies)
3. HDPA decisions showing identical conduct was sanctioned
4. Greek statutory text (current ΦΕΚ-anchored citations)
5. The opponent's own documentary admissions
6. EDPB guidelines (respected by courts; frame the legal standard)
7. ΕφΑθ decisions in the same circuit (high relevance)

### Useful Primarily for Ombudsman / HDPA

1. Foreign national-court decisions on tenancy or consumer law (non-EU-harmonized)
2. Comparative depreciation / valuation grids
3. Consumer reviews and complaint patterns (background only)
4. OSINT portfolio analysis (supports a business-pattern argument)

### Useful Only for Negotiation / Settlement

1. Reputational-risk framing (platform ratings)
2. Media framing
3. Foreign benchmarks as "industry practice" references
4. Arguments lacking a Greek legal basis but commercially reasonable

### Never Useful in Any Legal Forum

1. Google Maps, TripAdvisor, social-media posts as authority
2. Consumer-forum discussions
3. AI-generated opinions presented as authority
4. UK law post-Brexit
5. Unverified blog posts or unofficial legal summaries
